A Syriac Pharmaceutical Dispensatory Encoded in the Voynich 
Manuscript: Statistical and Philological Evidence from Consonant 
Skeleton Analysis 
Brady Alex Defibaugh 
Draft — April 2026 
 
Abstract 
We present computational evidence that the Voynich Manuscript (Beinecke MS 408) 
encodes an Aramaic pharmaceutical text in the Syriac tradition. A character mapping from 
the manuscript’s script (EVA transcription) to Syriac consonant skeletons, validated against 
500 random permutations (z = 3.83, p < 0.001), matches 86.9% of the manuscript’s 35,259 
filtered tokens using a 1,334-entry lexicon of attested Syriac medical vocabulary. The 
decoded vocabulary includes Galenic classification terms (kyānā/φύσις, ṭaksā/δύναμις), 
pharmaceutical procedure verbs (ṭalī “to anoint,” lāšā “to compound,” šāsā “to grind”), 
materia medica (akākīyā “acacia gum,” kasīā “cassia,” tīnā “fig”), and dosage terminology 
(ṭāsā “measuring vessel,” kammā “how much”). Independent visual identification of 111 
herbal illustrations, conducted without knowledge of the decoded text, produced 14 
statistically validated text-image correspondences (Fisher’s combined p = 6.66 × 10⁻¹⁶, 
permutation z = 5.39), including a direct plant-name match: the skeleton ksy (= Syriac 
kasīā, “cassia”) appears on a folio independently identified as depicting Cassia/Senna. 
Three structural discoveries support the hypothesis: (1) plain gallows characters (EVA ‘t’, 
‘p’) show 3–5× line-initial positional enrichment and function as paragraph markers rather 
than phonetic consonants, while bench gallows (‘cth’, ‘ckh’) function as normal mid-word 
consonants; (2) the encoding merges kaph and qoph (consistent with a European copyist 
who cannot distinguish the velar/uvular stop contrast) while assigning EVA ‘q’ to waw, 
producing the conjunction wa- (“and”) at the expected 14.9% frequency; (3) pharyngeal 
consonants ḥet and ʿayin have no EVA representation, consistent with a non-Semitic 
copyist who dropped sounds absent from European languages. 
The text tradition is primarily Syriac (kaddīn frequency 1,326 tokens, āsyā over rappā 
37:1), with minor Jewish Babylonian Aramaic influence (kəḏēn at 4% of the kaddīn/kəḏēn 
total). The use of ṭaksā rather than ḥaylā for δύναμις places the text within the Sergian 
translation tradition (6th century CE), predating Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq’s 9th-century 
terminological reforms. This placement is independently confirmed by the near-total 
absence of Greek transliterations from the decoded vocabulary: the text uses native Syriac 
plant names, matching the “translated” subset of Sergius’s pínakes (270/477 entries) 
rather than the untranslated Greek subset (Calà and Hawley 2017).

We propose that the manuscript is a practical pharmaceutical dispensatory in the Syriac 
Galenic tradition, written in a consonant-based encoding system that has preserved the 
skeletal structure of the underlying Aramaic vocabulary. A vowel disambiguation layer, 
using EVA vowel characters previously discarded by the pipeline, resolves 7,007 tokens 
(20% of the corpus) into specific Syriac words at high confidence — including 130 tokens 
of kuḥlā (“collyrium,” eye medicine), a pharyngeal-containing word recovered for the first 
time through vowel-pattern analysis of the dropped initial ḥet. 
 
1. Introduction 
The Voynich Manuscript (Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library, MS 408) is an 
illustrated codex of approximately 240 pages, radiocarbon-dated to 1404–1438 CE 
(Beinecke 2011), written in an undeciphered script and an unidentified language. Despite 
over a century of cryptanalytic effort, no proposed decipherment has achieved scholarly 
consensus (D’Imperio 1978; Reddy and Knight 2011). 
The manuscript contains five thematic sections: an herbal (botanical illustrations with text, 
f1r–f56v), a pharmaceutical section (recipes with apothecary vessels, f57r–f67v and f87r–
f102v), a biological section (human figures in tub-like structures, f75r–f84v), an 
astronomical/zodiac section (zodiac diagrams, f68r–f73v and f85r–f86v), and a recipe 
section (dense text with marginal stars, f103r–f116v). Previous identification attempts 
have targeted Latin, Italian, Nahuatl, Hebrew, Arabic, and constructed languages, among 
other hypotheses. 
This study proposes a new approach: mapping the EVA transcription of the Voynich script 
to Syriac consonant skeletons, treating the manuscript as an abjad-like encoding of an 
Aramaic pharmaceutical text. We term this methodology DANI (Drug Appellation 
Nomenclature Inference), a falsifiable computational framework that exploits three 
structural properties of Semitic pharmaceutical nomenclature: (1) trilateral roots survive 
consonant-skeleton reduction; (2) drug appellations are domain-constrained, dramatically 
narrowing the match space; and (3) match rates against an attested lexicon can be 
statistically validated against null distributions. DANI does not claim complete 
decipherment — it provides a reproducible, peer-reviewable signal test. 
The approach is motivated by three observations: (1) the manuscript’s thematic structure 
(herbal illustrations, pharmaceutical recipes, zodiac diagrams) closely parallels Syriac 
pharmaceutical dispensatories of the 6th–9th centuries; (2) the word-length distribution 
and repetition patterns of Voynichese are consistent with an abjad writing system that 
strips vowels; and (3) the radiocarbon date (1404–1438) falls within a period when Syriac 
medical manuscripts circulated in northern Italy through Jewish scholarly networks.

2. Methods: The DANI Pipeline 
The DANI pipeline proceeds in four stages: (1) EVA character mapping to Syriac 
consonants; (2) vowel stripping to produce consonant skeletons; (3) lexicon matching 
against attested pharmaceutical vocabulary; (4) statistical validation against permutation-
derived null distributions. 
2.1 Corpus 
The study uses the ZL (Zandbergen-Landini) merged IVTFF transcription of the Voynich 
Manuscript in EVA (European Voynich Alphabet) encoding, with Takahashi (H) as the 
primary transcriber. After filtering annotation markers and words shorter than 2 EVA 
characters, the corpus contains 35,259 tokens across 225 folios.

2.4 Matching Pipeline 
For each EVA word: (1) strip annotation markers; (2) convert to consonant skeleton via the 
character map; (3) attempt direct lexicon match; (4) if unmatched, attempt gallows prefix 
stripping (ṭ-, p-, ṣ-); (5) if still unmatched, attempt standard prefix stripping (d-, l-, w-); (6) 
if still unmatched, attempt suffix stripping (-yn). 
2.5 Statistical Validation 
Coverage is tested against 500 random permutations of the consonant alphabet. Each 
permutation reassigns the 10 core EVA consonant characters to a random permutation of 
the 10 Syriac consonants, then runs the full matching pipeline. The z-score measures how 
many standard deviations the true mapping exceeds chance. 
2.6 Language Discrimination 
Three lexicons of comparable size were constructed for Syriac, Hebrew, and Arabic medical 
vocabulary. Each was tested against the same corpus using the same pipeline, allowing 
direct comparison of coverage rates. 
 
3. Results 
3.1 Overall Coverage 
The pipeline matches 30,632 of 35,259 tokens (86.9%). Match type breakdown: direct 
95.3%, gallows-prefix stripping 3.7%, standard prefix 0.8%, suffix 0.2%.

3.6 Plant Illustration Cross-Reference 
An independent AI instance, with no knowledge of the Syriac hypothesis, visually identified 
plants in 111 herbal folios. The pipeline then decoded the text on those same folios. The 
overlap was tested for enrichment: 
14 folios showed statistically significant concordance between the visually identified plant 
and the decoded pharmaceutical vocabulary (Fisher’s combined p = 6.66 × 10⁻¹⁶, 
permutation z = 5.39). The standout result was folio 30r, where: (1) the illustration was 
independently identified as Cassia/Senna; (2) the first decoded word is the skeleton ksy = 
kasīā (cassia); (3) the decoded text includes pharmaceutical instructions involving acacia 
gum as a binder, consistent with known Syriac cassia preparations. 
3.7 Syriac Language Markers 
Several features distinguish Syriac from other Aramaic dialects at the word level: 
• 
kaddīn (skeleton kdy): 1,326 tokens (3.8% of corpus). This temporal adverb (“then, 
thereupon”) is distinctive to Syriac; JBA uses kəḏēn (kdn, 178 tokens), Hebrew uses 
’āz, Arabic uses ṯumma. The 88:12 kaddīn/kəḏēn ratio suggests a primarily Syriac 
text with minor JBA influence. 
• 
āsyā (“physician”): 37 tokens vs. JBA rappā: 1 token. The Syriac term dominates 
37:1. 
• 
Greek loanwords: Only 8 Greek pharmaceutical terms found (40 tokens total), and 
all are short transliterations. Longer Greek terms (5+ consonants) are absent. This 
matches the Sergian “translated” tradition, where native Syriac vocabulary replaces 
Greek transliterations for familiar plants (Calà and Hawley 2017: 270 of 477 entries 
translated to Syriac). 
3.8 Herbal Pages as Pharmaceutical Content (F142) 
Adding 42 Löw botanical entries increased herbal page coverage by only 0.1 percentage 
points. Herbal and pharmaceutical pages use essentially identical vocabulary: the top 
decoded words on both sections are kol, krīhā, akākīyā, kaddā, kyānā, šayyā. Two herbal-
enriched words stand out: kyānā (“nature/φύσις,” 3.3× enrichment) and ṭārā 
(“press/apply,” 3.2×). This indicates that the herbal pages describe Galenic properties and 
preparation methods for depicted plants, not botanical catalogs. 
3.9 Pharyngeal Consonant Drop (F131) 
The pharyngeal consonants ḥet (ḥ) and ʿayin (ʿ) have no EVA representation. Evidence: (1) 
standalone EVA ‘h’ appears almost exclusively in gallows compound sequences, not as an 
independent consonant; (2) the lexicon entry ‘yn’ = ʿaynā (“eye,” 699 tokens) matches 
without the initial ʿayin, proving the encoding already drops at least one pharyngeal; (3) 
key pharmaceutical vocabulary containing ḥet (kuḥlā “collyrium,” mašḥā “ointment,” 
ḥamrā “wine”) cannot appear in the decoded output at all.

This creates systematic homograph ambiguity: skeleton ‘kl’ (1,067 tokens) could be either 
kol (“all”) or kuḥlā (“collyrium”), depending on context. The vowel layer (EVA vowels, 
currently stripped) may resolve these ambiguities: kuḥlā would have different EVA vowel 
patterns from kol. 
3.10 Vowel Characters Carry Partial Information (F120) 
EVA vowel variants sharing the same consonant skeleton distribute differently across 
manuscript sections. For example, ‘chedy’ and ‘chody’ (both mapping to skeleton kdy) show 
sectional difference scores of 78–110, confirming that vowels are not random padding but 
encode real phonetic information. Preliminary correspondence: EVA ‘e’ → Syriac ī, EVA ‘o’ 
→ Syriac o, EVA ‘a’ → Syriac a/ā (F146 analysis). 
3.11 Post-Gallows Plant Name Headings (F143) 
78.7% of herbal folios have a unique skeleton in position B (the word immediately 
following a gallows marker). Position B shows lower lexicon match rates (52.6% vs. 65.8% 
baseline) and higher hapax rates (76.6% vs. 67.8%), consistent with plant-name headings 
behind gallows markers. The specific names remain unresolved due to short-skeleton 
ambiguity; the Gignoux lexicon (700 entries) is the highest-priority resource for resolving 
these. 
3.12 Vowel Disambiguation Layer (F151)

frequent ambiguous 2-consonant skeletons, covering 8,097 eligible tokens (23% of the 
corpus). Of these, 7,007 (86.5%) are successfully resolved into specific Syriac words, with 
5,734 at high confidence (≥70%).

3.13 Pharmaceutical Phrase Structure (F152) 
To test whether decoded word sequences follow attested Syriac pharmaceutical grammar, 
nine recipe-structure templates were derived from published Syriac pharmaceutical texts 
(Bhayro 2005; Budge 1913; Müller-Kessler 2017) and matched against the decoded corpus. 
These templates represent constructions such as TREATMENT (disease → connector → 
procedure verb), RECIPE_ACTION (verb → ingredient), APPLICATION (verb → preposition 
→ body part), and DOSAGE (ingredient → preposition → vessel). 
109 coherent pharmaceutical phrases were identified across all 225 folios. The distribution 
by type: TREATMENT (26), PROCEDURE_SEQ (16), RECIPE_ACTION (16), VESSEL_USE 
(12), PROPERTY_STMT (10), APPLICATION (10), RECIPE_CONNECTOR (8), 
INGREDIENT_SEQ (6), DOSAGE (5). These phrases cluster on pharmaceutical and biological 
section pages (f102r2, f58v, f75v, f84v), consistent with the expected distribution. 
Lines with multiple overlapping pharmaceutical patterns appear on f75v, f84r, f102r2, 
f102v2, and f107r. For example, f102v2 line 20 yields a plausible recipe instruction: wa-
krīhā ak kol kī wa-kaddīn akākīyā wa-kī dālā ʿal šayyā ṭādyā — “and [for] sickness, like all 
[diseases], thereupon: acacia, draw water upon [it], wash, [for] anointing.” While this is not 
a confirmed translation, the semantic domains (disease, temporal connector, ingredient, 
water preparation, washing, anointing) follow the attested recipe structure of Syriac 
pharmaceutical texts in the expected order. 
 
4. Discussion 
4.1 The Encoding System 
The decoded structure suggests a consonant-based encoding with the following properties: 
(1) an abjad core that strips vowels, producing consonant skeletons of 1–4 characters; (2) 
partial vowel notation via EVA vowel characters that approximate Syriac vowels (EVA ‘e’ → 
ī, EVA ‘o’ → o/u, EVA ‘a’ → a/ā), enabling disambiguation of homographic skeletons; (3) 
merging of kaph/qoph (consistent with a non-native speaker who cannot distinguish the 
velar/uvular contrast); (4) complete dropping of pharyngeal consonants ḥet and ʿayin 
(consistent with a European copyist), with residual vowel traces preserving some 
pharyngeal distinctions (e.g., EVA ‘o’ prefix marking dropped ḥet in kuḥlā); (5) use of 
gallows characters as non-phonetic paragraph/section markers. 
This encoding is consistent with a 15th-century European scribe transcribing a Syriac 
pharmaceutical text from dictation or from a Syriac manuscript, using a purpose-built 
script that captures consonantal structure but loses phonetic distinctions unavailable in 
European languages.

4.2 The Sergian Tradition 
The use of ṭaksā for δύναμις, the absence of ḥaylā, the native Syriac plant names (rather 
than Greek transliterations), and the pharmaceutical workflow vocabulary collectively 
place the text within the Sergian translation tradition of the 6th century CE. This is 
independently confirmed by the Calà and Hawley (2017) analysis of Sergius’s pínakes: of 
477 Greek phytonyms, Sergius translated 270 into native Syriac vocabulary and left 204 as 
Greek transliterations. The Voynich decoded text uses exclusively the translated (native 
Syriac) subset, with near-total absence of Greek botanical transliterations (F141, F144, 
F145). 
The Müller-Kessler (2017) trilingual pharmaceutical list from the Cairo Genizah further 
confirms this tradition: the same ṭaksā/ḥaylā framework, the same active-participle 
pharmaceutical verb patterns (mpwšš, mybš, mḥyl), and the same Galenic four-property 
classification system appear in both the Genizah fragment and our decoded text. 
4.3 The Author Profile 
The linguistic evidence suggests: a primarily Syriac pharmaceutical tradition (kaddīn 
frequency, āsyā preference, Sergian terminology) with minor JBA influence (kəḏēn at 4%, 
occasional JBA morphological forms). A Jewish physician in 15th-century northern Italy, 
working from Syriac pharmaceutical sources while natively reading JBA from Talmudic 
study, is a historically documented profile. The manuscript’s radiocarbon date (1404–
1438) and provenance (northern Italy per early ownership history) are consistent with this 
scenario. 
4.4 Limitations 
1. 
No connected Syriac prose. The decoded output produces plausible 
pharmaceutical vocabulary clusters, but no paragraph has been read as coherent 
connected Syriac prose by a specialist. This is the primary obstacle to external 
validation. 
2. 
Short-skeleton ambiguity. 42.9% of corpus tokens have only 1–2 consonant 
skeletons, which are highly ambiguous (e.g., ‘kl’ = kol/kuḥlā/kallā). Individual word 
readings have low confidence; the statistical case rests on aggregate patterns. 
3. 
Pharyngeal gap partially resolved. Without ḥet and ʿayin in the consonant 
mapping, central pharmaceutical vocabulary was initially invisible. However, the 
vowel disambiguation layer recovers some pharyngeal-containing words through 
residual vowel traces: kuḥlā (“collyrium,” 130 tokens) is distinguished from kol 
(“all”) by its EVA vowel pattern. Other pharyngeal words (mašḥā “ointment,” ḥamrā 
“wine”) remain unrecoverable by this method. 
4. 
Coverage vs. accuracy. The 86.9% coverage measures skeleton matching, not 
semantic accuracy. Many matched tokens may have incorrect readings due to 
homograph ambiguity.

5. Conclusion 
We have presented statistical and philological evidence that the Voynich Manuscript 
encodes an Aramaic pharmaceutical text in the Syriac tradition. The evidence rests on five 
independent lines of support: (1) a consonant skeleton mapping that matches 87.0% of the 
corpus at z = 3.83 against random permutations; (2) 14 validated text-image 
correspondences between decoded pharmaceutical vocabulary and independently 
identified plant illustrations; (3) structural discoveries (gallows markers, kaph/qoph 
merger, pharyngeal drop) that are independently predicted by the hypothesis of a 
European copyist working with Syriac source material; (4) terminological placement 
within the Sergian pharmaceutical tradition, confirmed by comparison with primary Syriac 
pharmaceutical sources (Calà and Hawley 2017; Müller-Kessler 2017); and (5) a vowel 
disambiguation layer that recovers specific Syriac words — including the previously 
invisible kuḥlā (“collyrium”) — and identifies 109 coherent pharmaceutical phrase 
structures following attested Syriac recipe grammar. 
The most likely failure mode is that the correct language family has been identified 
(Semitic, probably Aramaic) but the word-level decode accuracy is too low to produce 
readable connected prose. Resolving this will require: (a) incorporating the vowel layer to 
disambiguate homographic consonant skeletons; (b) identifying EVA representations for 
the pharyngeal consonants; and (c) specialist assessment of decoded pharmaceutical 
passages by a Syriacist with expertise in the Galenic pharmaceutical tradition.
