Abstract 
We apply semantic attractor analysis — a method combining sentence-transformer embeddings, 
DBSCAN clustering, and recurrence matrix visualization — to the Voynich Manuscript (Beinecke 
MS 408), using the Currier/FSG transcription (voyn_101.txt). The pipeline, originally developed 
for conversational semantic analysis, was adapted for an unknown symbolic system by replacing 
character n-gram embeddings with word-level TF-IDF embeddings treating each Voynich token 
as an atomic unit. 
 
The analysis reveals: (1) Zipf's law compliance consistent with natural language (slope -0.768, R² 
= 0.875); (2) 190 distinct semantic attractors from constrained token recombination; (3) 
statistically significant spatial separation of manuscript sections in embedding space; and (4) a 
semantic recurrence topology consistent with inhabited, recursive textual spaces rather than 
forward-directed transmission or ideological lock-in. 
 
These findings are inconsistent with hoax or random generation hypotheses. The structural 
signature most closely resembles operator-based generative systems — in which a finite token 
set produces meaning through constrained recombination and positional inflection — rather than 
substitution ciphers or natural prose. We propose that Voynich token morphology encodes 
operator functions through prefixation and infixation patterns, structurally analogous to 
independently developed systems such as Operator Abjad (OA). 
 
Keywords: Voynich Manuscript, semantic attractor analysis, generative symbolic systems, 
operator-based languages, recurrence matrix, Zipf's law, manuscript authentication

1. Introduction 
The Voynich Manuscript (MS 408, Beinecke Rare Book Library, Yale University) remains one of 
the most studied undecoded documents in history. Dating to the early 15th century by radiocarbon 
analysis, it comprises approximately 240 vellum pages of unknown script accompanied by 
illustrations conventionally categorized into herbal, astronomical, biological, cosmological, 
pharmaceutical, and recipe sections. 
 
Prior analytical approaches have pursued three primary hypotheses: (1) natural language written 
in an unknown or extinct script; (2) a cipher or steganographic encoding of a known language; (3) 
a constructed language or hoax. Statistical analyses have repeatedly confirmed that Voynich text 
exhibits Zipf's law compliance and entropy characteristics consistent with natural language, 
complicating the hoax hypothesis without resolving the cipher versus natural language question. 
 
The present study applies a novel analytical framework — semantic attractor analysis — not 
previously applied to the Voynich Manuscript. This framework, derived from dynamical systems 
theory and applied to textual corpora, identifies stable semantic basins (attractors) in high-
dimensional embedding space, maps recurrence patterns, and characterizes the topological 
structure of the text's semantic manifold. The central question is not what the Voynich says, but 
what shape its meaning-space has — and whether that shape is consistent with intentional 
semantic organization. 
1.1 The Operator Hypothesis 
This analysis was motivated in part by parallel development of Operator Abjad (OA), a purpose-
constructed ritual-compositional language built on the following principles: a finite set of 
consonantal roots encodes core meaning; operators govern recombination and transformation of 
roots; emergent compounds produce new meaning through constrained combination; and 
stabilized forms (glyphs) represent compressed, high-frequency meanings. 
 
OA's core principle — roots encode, operators express, compounds emerge, glyphs stabilize — 
describes a generative meaning-engine rather than a descriptive language. The present analysis 
tests whether Voynich token morphology exhibits structural properties consistent with this class 
of system. 
 
"The Voynich Manuscript may encode a symbolic system where meaning is generated 
through constrained recombination of a finite token set, similar in structural class to 
operator-based systems like OA — but with operators either implicit, encoded, or collapsed 
into token morphology."

2.3 Embedding Strategy 
Standard semantic embedding models (trained on English prose) are inappropriate for unknown 
symbolic systems. We employed word-level TF-IDF vectorization with the following parameters: 
analyzer='word'; ngram_range=(1,2); min_df=2; max_features=10,000; sublinear_tf=True; 
token_pattern matching any non-whitespace sequence. This approach treats each Voynich token 
as a unit of analysis, finding lines with similar token distributions without imposing external 
semantic priors. 
 
For comparison, standard sentence-transformer embeddings (all-mpnet-base-v2) were also 
computed, providing a baseline for English-model response to the unknown text. 
2.4 Attractor Detection 
DBSCAN clustering (eps=0.5, min_samples=2, metric=cosine) was applied to L2-normalized 
embeddings. Parameters were tuned from standard conversational corpus values (eps=0.3,

The Zipf distribution is particularly notable. A slope of -0.768 with R²=0.875 represents excellent

forms through recombination. This is a characteristic signature of agglutinative or operator-based 
systems rather than analytic languages with fixed vocabulary.

The Voynich attractor count (190) exceeds all comparison texts, including Dante's Inferno (74). 
This is not consistent with hoax generation, which produces low attractor counts, nor with 
ideological lock-in, which produces single supermassive basins. A text generated randomly or

vocabulary differentiation across the corpus. 
3.3 Section Separation in Embedding Space 
The most significant finding of this analysis is the spatial separation of manuscript sections in 
word-embedding space. When lines are colored by their conventional section assignment (herbal 
A, astronomical, biological, cosmological, herbal B, pharmaceutical), clear regional clustering is 
visible in the 2D t-SNE projection. 
 
Specifically: herbal A lines cluster in one region; pharmaceutical lines cluster in a distinct region; 
biological lines occupy a third region; astronomical lines form a fourth cluster. This section 
separation occurs purely on the basis of token distribution — the algorithm has no knowledge of 
the manuscript's visual organization or scholarly section assignments. 
 
This result constitutes strong evidence against the hoax hypothesis. A hoaxer producing 
meaningless text would have no mechanism for maintaining consistent domain-specific 
vocabulary across hundreds of pages in a way that produces statistically significant clustering. 
The section separation implies that whoever produced the Voynich was encoding genuinely 
different content in different sections — content with different token inventories.

The semantic recurrence matrix (cosine similarity across the full corpus) exhibits a warm, textured 
green pattern with block structure, most closely resembling the personal grimoire corpus rather 
than the directed-transmission signature of Revelation or the lock-in signature of the Manifesto. 
 
This topology — inhabited space with deep structural coherence under surface variety — is 
characteristic of texts designed for recursive engagement rather than one-time transmission. The 
text recognizes itself across distance, suggesting consistent underlying structural principles 
throughout. 
 
The n-gram recurrence matrix is nearly featureless (dark blue), indicating that the manuscript 
does not repeat exact token sequences — consistent with a generative system producing novel 
surface forms from a stable deep structure.

4. Discussion 
4.1 Against the Hoax Hypothesis 
The combination of findings presented here is structurally inconsistent with hoax generation. A 
successful hoax requires: (1) Zipf compliance — achievable through careful construction but rare 
in genuine hoaxes; (2) high attractor count — not achievable through random or repetitive 
generation; (3) section-specific vocabulary clustering — requires sustained domain-specific 
consistency across hundreds of pages.

Most critically, the section separation result cannot easily be faked. To produce herbal vocabulary 
that clusters distinctly from astronomical vocabulary in a word-embedding space, a hoaxer would 
need to maintain statistically significant differences in token usage across sections — essentially 
constructing a multi-domain symbolic system. At that point, the distinction between hoax and 
genuine constructed language becomes definitional rather than substantive. 
4.2 The Generative System Hypothesis 
The structural signature most consistent with the Voynich data is that of an operator-based 
generative system. The defining characteristics of such a system are: a finite root vocabulary; 
operators governing recombination; high surface variety from low deep complexity; domain-
specific operator deployment; and morphological productivity (high hapax ratio). 
 
All five characteristics are present in the Voynich data. The 190 attractors represent the semantic 
basins generated by combining roots with operators across different contextual domains. The 
section separation represents domain-specific operator deployment — the same root inventory 
being combined differently in herbal versus astronomical versus pharmaceutical contexts. 
 
This interpretation does not require that we identify the specific operators or roots. It predicts that: 
(1) the highest-frequency tokens will prove to be operators rather than content words; (2) 
systematic analysis of token positional patterns will reveal a small operator set; (3) once operators 
are identified, root vocabulary will be significantly smaller than current apparent vocabulary 
suggests. 
4.3 Structural Isomorphism with Operator Abjad 
Operator Abjad (OA), an independently developed ritual-compositional language, shares the 
following structural properties with the Voynich system as characterized by this analysis: finite 
consonantal roots encoding core meaning; operators governing transformation and 
recombination; emergent compounds as the primary meaning-generation mechanism; glyph 
stabilization of high-frequency forms. 
 
The structural isomorphism does not imply historical connection. Rather, it suggests that operator-
based generative systems represent a discoverable design space for symbolic languages — one 
that can be arrived at independently by practitioners working toward similar goals: maximum 
semantic compression, morphological productivity, and resistance to casual decoding. 
 
The Voynich author and independent modern practitioners appear to have converged on similar 
architectural solutions to similar problems. This convergence, if confirmed by deeper analysis, 
would constitute evidence that operator-based generative structure represents a robust and 
perhaps optimal design for esoteric or ritual symbolic systems. 
4.4 Limitations 
Several significant limitations constrain interpretation of these results. First, the Currier/FSG 
transcription system introduces its own analytical layer between the original manuscript and the

analysis. Different transcription systems may produce different results. Second, the pipeline 
parameters (eps, min_samples) were tuned heuristically; systematic parameter optimization may 
alter attractor counts. Third, the section boundaries used for the section separation analysis are 
conventional assignments, not manuscript-internal designations. Fourth, and most importantly, 
this analysis characterizes structural properties of the Voynich without making claims about 
content. The identification of generative structure does not constitute decipherment. 
 
 
 
5. Conclusions 
Semantic attractor analysis of the Voynich Manuscript produces results inconsistent with hoax or 
random generation and consistent with an intentional operator-based generative symbolic 
system. The key findings are: 
 
1. Zipf's law compliance (slope -0.768, R²=0.875) confirms non-random token distribution. 
2. 190 semantic attractors indicate genuine vocabulary differentiation beyond hoax-level 
complexity. 
3. Statistically significant section separation in embedding space implies domain-specific 
content organization. 
4. Inhabited recurrence topology suggests recursive structural coherence rather than one-
time transmission. 
5. Token morphology patterns are consistent with an operator-prefix system modifying a 
finite root vocabulary. 
 
We propose that future analysis focus on identifying the operator set through positional frequency 
analysis of token prefixes, and testing the prediction that apparent vocabulary size reduces 
significantly once operators are separated from roots. 
 
The structural isomorphism with independently developed operator-based systems suggests that 
this architectural class represents a convergent solution to the design problem of maximum 
semantic compression with high morphological productivity — a design space discoverable by 
practitioners working across centuries without direct contact.
